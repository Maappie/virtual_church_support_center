About Us

The history of San Miguel de Manila, possibly the first parish in the Philippines dedicated to the Archangel, spans centuries of transformation and resilience. Established in the 1620s in Dilao, east of Intramuros, it was initially a Jesuit effort to unite Japanese immigrants in their own quarter. The church's construction and relocation, recounted in accounts from the time, reflect the tumultuous relations between Manila and Japan. Over the years, jurisdictional disputes arose between the Franciscan and Jesuit orders regarding ministering to the Japanese settlers. The parish saw various calamities, including plagues, earthquakes, and fires, yet it endured.

In 1768, the expulsion of the Jesuits left San Miguel without pastors, leading to its attachment to other parishes until eventually falling under Franciscan care. Catastrophes like the great fire of 1778 prompted the parish's relocation across the Pasig River. Reconstruction efforts and renovations followed, often financed by patrons like the Roxas de Ayalas

San Miguel's significance grew, becoming a district of Manila and serving as the Pro-cathedral after the inauguration of the Republic in 1946. The church witnessed notable events in Philippine history, such as the elevation of Archbishop Vicente Reyes to the episcopate. It remained a focal point for society events, including weddings and ceremonies, attracting prominent figures like President Carlos P. Garcia.

Despite political upheavals, including Martial Law under Marcos, the parish persisted, adapting to challenges. Msgr. Josefino Ramirez introduced street masses to reach out to communities. His vision for San Miguel as a National Shrine dedicated to St. Michael and the Archangels came to fruition in 1986, coinciding with the EDSA Revolution.

The consecration of the church as the National Shrine marked a new chapter, symbolizing resilience, faith, and community amidst historical and social change.
